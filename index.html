Introduction

The wonders of the world are almost legendary buildings that have marked the soul of man, even in his language. This classification recognizes the technical prowess of the ancient world, as well as the constructions of man endowed with an impressive beauty or greatness that imbue the imagination of men with a fascination that endures even today.

7 Wonders of the modern world

Colosseum - Italy

The Colosseum or Flavius Amphitheater is an amphitheater of the Roman Empire, built in the first century and located in the center of the city of Rome. Its original name, Flavian Amphitheater, refers to the Flavian dynasty of emperors that built it; its later name, Colosseum, and for which it is better known today, is due to a large statue that was nearby, the Colossus of Nero, which has not reached us. Because of its conservation and history, the Colosseum is one of the most famous monuments of classical antiquity.

  • It was declared a World Heritage Site in 1980 by UNESCO.
  • It was declared one of the new seven wonders of the modern world on July 7, 2007.

Chichén Itzá - México

It is one of the main archaeological sites of the Yucatan Peninsula in Mexico. It is located in the municipality of Tinum, in the state of Yucatan. It consisted of a city or a ceremonial center, which went through various constructive periods and influences of the different peoples who occupied it and that promoted it since its foundation.

  • It was inscribed on the World Heritage List by UNESCO in 1988.
  • On July 7, 2007 it was recognized as one of the new seven wonders of the modern world.

Christ the Redeemer - Brazil

The Christ Redeemer or Christ of the Corcovado is a huge statue of Jesus of Nazareth with open arms showing the city of Rio de Janeiro, Brazil. The statue has a height of 30.1 meters on a pedestal of 8 meters. This monument was inaugurated on October 12, 1931, after approximately five years of construction. Considering the second largest Art Deco statue in the world.

  • On July 7, 2007 it was recognized as one of the new seven wonders of the modern world.

Machu Picchu - Perú

Machu Picchu is the contemporary name given to a llaqta (ancient Andean Inca settlement) built before the 15th century, located in the Eastern Cordillera of southern Peru, in the Andes mountain range at 2430 meters above sea level. It is located in the region of Cusco, province of Urubamba, district of Machupicchu, on the Sacred Valley of the Incas, 80 kilometers northwest of the city of Cusco, city of Peru and where the Urubamba River flows, a river that crosses the mountain range and originates a canyon with a tropical mountain climate. According to many scholars its original name would have been Llaqtapata.

  • Machu Picchu was declared a Peruvian Historic Sanctuary in 1981.
  • Has been on the UNESCO World Heritage List since 1983.
  • On July 7, 2007 Machu Picchu was declared one of the new seven wonders of the modern world.

Great Wall of China - China

The Great Wall of China is an ancient Chinese fortification built and reconstructed between the 5th century BC and the 16th century (Modern Age) to protect the northern border of the Chinese Empire during the successive imperial dynasties from the attacks of the Xiongnu nomads of Mongolia and Manchuria. Counting its ramifications and secondary constructions, it is estimated that it is about 21 200 kilometers long, from the border with Korea, at the edge of the Yalu River, to the Gobi Desert, along an arc that outlines approximately the southern edge of Inner Mongolia, although only 30% of it is preserved today. On average, it is 6 to 7 meters high and 4 to 5 meters wide.

  • The wall was designated a World Heritage Site by UNESCO in 1987.
  • In 2007 it was announced that the Chinese wall was chosen as one of the winners in the list of the New Seven Wonders of the Modern World.

Petra - Jordan

Petra is an important archaeological site in Jordan, and the capital of the ancient Nabataean kingdom, whose inhabitants called it Raqmu. The name of Petra comes from the Greek πέτρα that means stone, and its name is perfectly suitable; it is not a city built with stone but, literally, excavated and sculpted in the stone. The settlement of Petra is located in a narrow valley, east of the Arava Valley that extends from the Dead Sea to the Gulf of Aqaba. The most famous remains of Petra are undoubtedly its constructions carved into the very rock of the valley (hemispheres), in particular, the buildings known as the Khazneh (the Treasure) and the Deir (the Monastery).

  • Since December 6, 1985 it has been inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage List.
  • Since July 7, 2007, Petra is part of the new seven wonders of the modern world.

Taj Mahal - India

The Taj Mahal is a funeral monument built between 1631 and 1654 in the city of Agra, state of Uttar Pradesh (India), on the banks of the Yamuna River, by the Muslim emperor Shah Jahan of the Mughal dynasty. The imposing complex of buildings was erected in honor of his favorite wife, Arjumand Banu Begum better known as Mumtaz Mahal who died in childbirth of her fourteenth child.

  • In 1983, it was recognized by UNESCO as a World Heritage Site.
  • In 2007 it was designated one of the New Seven Wonders of the Modern World.
7 Wonders of the ancient world

The Great Pyramid of Giza

Finished around the year 2570 B.C., it was built for the Pharaoh Cheops. Located in Giza, Egypt, it is the oldest, largest and most durable.

Hanging Gardens of Babylon

Built in 605 B.C.-562 B.C. located in the city of Babylon, current Iraq. They lasted until no more than 126 B.C., when the city was definitively destroyed by the Persians. It is the wonder that raises more doubts about its existence.

The Temple of Artemis.

In Ephesus (present-day Turkey) started to rise by King Croesus. Raised about 550 B.C. and destroyed by an intentional fire in 356 B.C., Alexander the Great ordered its reconstruction, which was completed after his death in 323 B.C. Antipater considered it the most impressive work on his list by far.

Statue of Zeus at Olympia

Sculpted around 430 BC by Phidias. Located inside the temple dedicated to Zeus himself in the city that hosts the famous games.

Mausoleum at Halicarnassus

Begun by the satrap Mausolo and continued by his wife Artemisia towards 353 B.C. in the city of Halicarnassus ( present Turkey ). It is assumed that it stood out from the other buildings because of its height and the white color of the materials used.5 Its name became synonymous with a great funeral monument.

Colossus of Rhodes

Forged between 294 B.C. and 282 B.C. by Cares de Lindos and located in the port of the city of Rhodes on the island of the same name, Greece, after the Rhodians defeated Demetrius Poliorcetes.

Lighthouse of Alexandria

It was built between 285 B.C. and 247 B.C. on the island of Faros, at the entrance of Alexandria (Egypt), to guide the ships that went to the two ports that the city had. Like the tomb of Mausolo, the Pharos tower did the same with the buildings constructed to help navigation by means of fixed lights on an elevated site.

The selection of the 7 wonders of the modern world

The selection of the 7 wonders of the world today was a way of updating the 7 wonders of the ancient age of which, as we saw earlier, only one remains (the Great Pyramid of Giza). Why are there 7 specifically? Because of the Hellenistic culture, which considered the number 7 as the perfect figure. The 7 wonders of the modern world were chosen in 2007 through an international public contest in which more than 100 million people from all over the world participated. Voting was done by SMS and Internet (specifically by registration by email).

The seventh election caused some commotion since official entities such as UNESCO, the United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization and who is in charge of declaring the selected sites as World Heritage, did not endorse the selection. In addition, nothing prevented a person from voting more than once, as long as they used another cell phone or email. Nevertheless, the seven wonders of the world that were established were the ones we have today. And, without a doubt, they are spectacular places worth visiting.

With nine days to go before the winners of the New 7 Wonders of the World contest are announced in Lisbon, Portugal, the controversy does not cease. The discussions are not only due to the close competition among the 20 man-made works that have reached the final of this contest, which began in 1999 thanks to Swiss-Canadian millionaire Bernard Weber. So much has been the furore among the public, that until a few days ago nearly 50 million people had voted on the website www.new7wonders.com, by phone and via text messages.

The contest organizers even announced several days ago that they had removed the ranking from their website because the positions of the candidates were constantly changing due to a sharp increase in the volume of voting. Initially, the sites voted for by the public were 77, from the nearly 200 that had been nominated by Weber, president of the New 7 Wonders Foundation, based in Zurich (Switzerland). A panel of architectural experts, chaired by Federico Mayor, former director of the UN Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization (UNESCO), then reduced the list to 21 potential wonders.

The friction began this year, when Egypt banned the image of the pyramids of Giza (the only one of the ancient wonders still standing) from appearing in the advertising of the New 7 Wonders, arguing that they were already a wonder. The organization of the contest determined that they could not be voted for and that they would be catalogued as "honorary candidates" of the contest. In spite of the inconvenience, the publicity increased and the governments of China and Brazil invited their citizens to vote for their candidates - the Great Wall and Christ the Redeemer, respectively - and even private enterprise got mixed up in the affair. In Mexico, Coca-Cola printed soda cans with legends asking people to choose the Chichen Itza pyramid as one of the seven wonders. Despite the boom it has had, Weber's idea suffered the most recent blow in that election week, when UNESCO cut off any link to the contest. This organization argued that it was doing so in order to avoid a "regrettable confusion" between its mandate to protect world heritage and the "media campaign" undertaken "in a private capacity" by Weber, which "will not be able to contribute in a significant and lasting way to the preservation of the sites chosen by the public". UNESCO also stressed that the election of the new seven wonders will be made only with the votes of the population that has access to the Internet and not of the whole planet. In any case, with voices for and against, the remaining nine days will be key for the world to know what their New Seven Wonders will be.
